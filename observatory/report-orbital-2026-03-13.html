1. Mantis Space Raises $10M for Orbital Power Infrastructure

Mantis Space, an Albuquerque-based startup, emerged from stealth March 12 with a $10 million seed round led by Rule 1 Ventures and Montauk Capital to develop orbital power transmission infrastructure addressing a fundamental constraint on LEO satellite operations: approximately one-third of orbital time occurs in Earth's shadow, where solar panels generate zero power. The company plans a constellation operating in low medium-Earth orbit that transmits power directly to satellites via laser-based optical systems, enabling continuous operations in mission-critical orbits without designing around eclipse constraints (SpaceNews, March 12; Satellite Today, March 12).

CEO Eric Truitt, former Terran Orbital chief solutions officer and PredaSAR co-founder, frames the problem as architectural: "We design around it. We fly around it. We make all these concessions, but the reality is we're putting this Band-Aid on the problem." Mantis Space's solution involves building optical payloads—not complete satellites—that partner spacecraft manufacturers will integrate. The satellites will fit on existing launch vehicles from SpaceX, Firefly Aerospace, Rocket Lab, Blue Origin, and Relativity; Truitt emphasized "we don't have to wait for Starship." New Mexico and the city of Albuquerque awarded approximately $24 million in incentives to relocate the company's headquarters and 2,000-square-meter manufacturing facility from Georgia. Mantis Space is establishing a Silicon Valley office targeting former Google, Apple, and Meta engineers and expanding from its current 20-person team.

The technical team includes John Sandusky (former Sandia National Laboratory space/solar/laser programs lead), Greg Brady (Apple Face ID optical systems designer), and Quentin Diduck (former Google MicroLED engineer). Co-founders include Hugh Wyman Howard III (retired Rear Admiral, former National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency operations director) and Jeremy Scheerer (former USAF program manager, MapLarge VP of defense systems). Admiral James Winnefeld Jr., former vice chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff and Rule 1 Ventures general partner, stated: "As the orbital economy matures, the limiting factor shifts from launch to performance. Mantis Space is addressing one of the last unbuilt layers of space infrastructure."

The direct relevance to orbital data centers is structural: continuous power access enables satellites to remain in operationally valuable orbits regardless of solar exposure. Current designs force constellation operators to either carry oversized battery systems to survive eclipse periods or position satellites in sun-synchronous orbits that compromise coverage geometry. If Mantis Space's power transmission proves viable, orbital data center architectures could prioritize computational performance and thermal management over solar array sizing and battery mass—potentially enabling denser computing clusters in specific orbital regimes without sacrificing uptime. The $10 million validates investor conviction that orbital power infrastructure constitutes necessary enabling technology for advanced space operations, including but not limited to computation.

Sources: SpaceNews, Satellite Today

2. Legal Scholars Warn of "Digital Soil" Governance Crisis

JURIST published a legal analysis March 12 by Vishal Sharma, an AI governance advocate specializing in data privacy and cyber law, arguing that SpaceX's orbital data center proposal exposes a "dangerous governance vacuum" where 1967 space treaties and Earth-bound privacy laws create what he terms a "Jurisdictional Mirage." The core crisis stems from Article VI of the Outer Space Treaty, which holds that satellites remain under the "authorization and continuing supervision" of their Launching State. For simple hardware, this established clear liability. For data processing infrastructure, it creates legal superposition: the United States retains physical jurisdiction over satellite hardware under the OST, while data itself remains subject to the sovereignty of users' home nations under regulations like India's DPDP Act or Europe's GDPR (JURIST, March 12).

Sharma introduces the concept of "Digital Soil"—territory defined not by geography but by control over computational foundations. He argues: "If a data breach occurs 500 kilometers above the Earth, which regulator has the standing to issue a warrant? A terrestrial court cannot seize an orbital server, and the Launching State has no treaty obligation to enforce foreign privacy laws." This produces what he calls "Legal Ghost Ships"—high-performance processors operating in permanent "offshore" status beyond democratic audit or subpoena. The analysis contends that tech giants situating AI models in orbital environments immune to sovereign oversight are effectively "seceding from the terrestrial social contract," establishing digital territory with no clear jurisdictional controls.

The piece extends the governance gap to lunar operations, warning of a "Lunar Jurisdictional Trap" where permanent settlements relying entirely on orbital AI for life support, navigation, and resource allocation would face catastrophic vulnerability if control infrastructure remains legally ambiguous. Sharma proposes two remedies: first, UN designation of specific orbital strata as "International Data Commons" governed by multilateral frameworks similar to Antarctica or the High Seas; second, mandatory "Agentic Compliance"—cryptographically secured observer algorithms built into hardware providing real-time, verifiable proof of compliance with Earth-bound privacy laws. He argues this is "the only way to pierce the veil of the Legal Ghost Ship" since regulators cannot physically board orbital servers to conduct audits.

The analysis represents the first comprehensive legal framework proposal specifically addressing orbital data center governance since the SpaceX FCC filing. Previous commentary focused on environmental impacts, collision risks, or economic viability; Sharma's piece targets the jurisdictional architecture enabling data sovereignty evasion. The "Digital Soil" framing reframes orbital infrastructure not as physical hardware subject to launch-state control but as geopolitical territory where computational sovereignty determines power independent of terrestrial borders. Whether international regulators adopt this framework remains uncertain, but the piece establishes a conceptual vocabulary for debates that previously lacked precise legal terminology.

3. Astronomers Quantify Million-Satellite Threat to Ground Observatories

Space.com published March 13 an assessment by John Barentine, astronomer and dark sky consultant, quantifying the astronomical impact of SpaceX's proposed one-million-satellite orbital data center constellation. Barentine estimates the constellation would produce "possibly tens of thousands of moving objects as bright as stars that are visible in the night sky at any given moment, even to the naked eye," representing a "vastly different prospect" compared to existing megaconstellations. Each orbital data center could measure up to 330 feet (100 meters) long, operating in pole-to-pole orbits at altitudes between 310 and 1,243 miles (500-2,000 km) with constant solar exposure—meaning full illumination at midnight as seen from ground observatories (Space.com, March 13).

The critical distinction from Starlink: current satellites orbit at lower altitudes and inclinations, spending most time in Earth's shadow and appearing dim or invisible during mid-night observations. SpaceX's data center proposal places satellites in high-inclination orbits ensuring continuous sunlight exposure regardless of time of night. Barentine described this as "a complete reversal" of progress made since 2019, when consultations with SpaceX led to brightness mitigation efforts—less reflective materials, tilted solar panels—that reduced Starlink satellite visibility to just above the International Astronomical Union's recommended threshold. The data center constellation threatens to "undermine what we have achieved in the last few years."

Operational impacts on major observatories would be severe. The $10 billion Vera Rubin Observatory (opened 2025) and $2 billion Extremely Large Telescope (under construction in Chile) would face continuous satellite streaks requiring either scheduling observations to avoid transits or closing camera shutters during passes. Barentine noted: "At some point, the amount of time the shutter is closed starts degrading your observations. With more than a million objects, the shutter would be closed more than it would be open." Additionally, with projected satellite replacement cycles, one spacecraft would burn up in Earth's atmosphere every three minutes, depositing aluminum oxide, lithium, and other metals into the upper atmosphere with potential ozone depletion and temperature impacts.

The FCC placed the SpaceX application on a fast-track path, eliminating environmental impact assessment requirements. Barentine explained that the process now presumes applications should be approved unless objectors conduct time-consuming analyses proving harm—a reversal from previous requirements that applicants demonstrate developments would not cause significant environmental damage. The objection deadline was March 6, compressing response time for astronomers and environmental groups. The piece represents the first detailed quantitative assessment from the professional astronomy community specifically addressing orbital data center brightness and operational interference since the FCC filing became public.

Source: Space.com

4. FCC Chair Dismisses Amazon's SpaceX Objections

FCC Chairman Brendan Carr publicly dismissed Amazon's petition opposing SpaceX's orbital data center application in statements March 11, telling Reuters he doesn't expect the objection to "get much traction" and posting on X that "Amazon should focus on the fact that it will fall roughly 1,000 satellites short of meeting its upcoming deployment milestone, rather than spending their time and resources filing petitions against companies that are putting thousands of satellites in orbit" (CNBC, March 11). The statement marks the first direct regulatory response to Amazon's March 6 filing, which characterized SpaceX's application as "a lofty ambition rather than a real plan" and urged the FCC to reject permission for the one-million-satellite constellation.

Carr, a longtime SpaceX supporter who previously accused the Biden-era FCC of "regulatory harassment" when the agency found Starlink WiFi unfit for a rural broadband program, framed Amazon's objection as commercial rivalry rather than substantive technical concern. Amazon requested a waiver or 24-month extension to July 2028 for deploying approximately 1,600 Leo (formerly Kuiper) satellites by its July 2026 deadline, citing rocket shortage and manufacturing disruptions. The FCC approved a separate petition in February allowing Amazon to deploy 4,500 additional satellites, more than doubling its planned constellation. SpaceX's Starlink currently operates approximately 9,000 satellites serving 9 million customers and recently received authorization for another 7,500 satellites.

Amazon's petition cited concerns from astronomers and environmental groups, arguing SpaceX's application "risks worsening international backlash" from regulators concerned about monopolization of space resources and warned that approval would force "every other operator in Low-Earth Orbit to plan around a constellation that may never exist, distorting international spectrum and orbital coordination proceedings, and lending regulatory legitimacy to what amounts to a publicity and narrative-shaping exercise." Carr's response suggests the FCC's current leadership views deployment pace as the primary metric for evaluating satellite operator credibility rather than environmental or astronomical impact assessments.

The exchange clarifies regulatory dynamics: Amazon's objection, however technically substantive, faces skepticism from an FCC chair who prioritizes commercial deployment over precautionary environmental review. Scientists warning of light pollution, orbital debris, and Kessler syndrome risks find their concerns filtered through competitive commercial framing. Carr's public dismissal before the FCC formally evaluates Amazon's petition signals the agency's likely trajectory on orbital data center applications—approval contingent on technical spectrum coordination rather than comprehensive environmental or astronomical impact review.

Source: CNBC

5. China Rejects Orbital Data Center Model

Gao Wen, computer scientist at Peking University and Chinese Academy of Engineering member, stated March 9 during China's "two sessions" legislative meetings that China should not follow SpaceX's orbital data center approach, instead prioritizing in-orbit satellite data processing. Gao, also a deputy to the National People's Congress, argued that electricity demand is not a major bottleneck for AI data centers in China—the primary justification for SpaceX's orbital infrastructure—making space-based general computing economically unjustified. "If processing and analysis can be done in orbit first, satellites could send back only the useful information. That would allow the data gathered in space to be used much more effectively," Gao told Sina News (SCMP, March 9).

The statement represents China's first senior-level technical assessment of orbital data centers since SpaceX's FCC filing and implicitly rejects the power/cooling economic thesis driving Western proposals. China's approach emphasizes edge computing for satellite constellations—processing Earth observation, reconnaissance, and communications data in orbit to reduce downlink bandwidth rather than deploying general-purpose AI training infrastructure in space. This aligns with China's previously announced 200,000-satellite constellation focused on "state coordination, data sovereignty, and in-orbit processing for secure, time-critical applications" rather than commercial cloud services.

The divergence suggests fundamentally different architectural philosophies: SpaceX/Western proposals treat orbit as an extension of terrestrial data center infrastructure, moving compute to where power and cooling are abundant; China's model treats orbit as a specialized edge environment where local processing reduces ground infrastructure load but general computing remains Earth-based. Gao's comments also reflect China's strategic emphasis on data sovereignty—in-orbit processing for Chinese satellites ensures data never leaves state-controlled infrastructure, while reliance on commercial orbital data centers (likely U.S.-operated) would introduce foreign dependencies.

The technical skepticism from a Chinese Academy of Engineering member carries weight: Gao is not a policy critic but a computer scientist questioning whether orbital data centers solve problems China actually faces. If electricity and cooling constraints don't justify the cost/complexity of orbital computing in China's assessment, the orbital data center market may fragment along geopolitical lines—U.S./Western operators pursuing power-constrained AI training infrastructure, China prioritizing sovereign in-orbit satellite data processing. This bifurcation would complicate international coordination on orbital resource allocation and spectrum management.

Source: SCMP

6. Research Update: LEO Semantic Communication Optimization

A paper published March 12 on arXiv (2603.12027) addresses power optimization for LEO satellite Earth observation missions, though not directly focused on orbital data centers. "A Joint JSCC-Resource Allocation Framework for QoS-Aware Semantic Communication in LEO Satellite-based EO Missions" proposes semantic communication methods with joint source-channel coding (JSCC) to reduce transmission power for high-resolution satellite imagery under LEO movement dynamics. The work, accepted for IEEE ICC 2026, aims to minimize total transmit power by jointly optimizing encoder-decoder parameters and resource allocation while maintaining image reconstruction quality thresholds. The JCRRA (joint compression ratio-resource allocation) algorithm demonstrates substantial power savings compared to greedy algorithms and conventional transmission paradigms (arXiv, March 12).

While not directly applicable to orbital data center proposals, the paper addresses a related infrastructure challenge: LEO satellites face power budget constraints and dynamic link conditions requiring efficient data transmission. If orbital data centers process satellite-generated imagery in orbit, the same semantic communication and compression optimization techniques would apply to inter-satellite links distributing workloads across computing clusters. The research contributes to the broader orbital computing stack by validating that intelligent compression and adaptive resource allocation can significantly reduce power consumption for data-intensive orbital operations—a critical constraint for any large-scale LEO computing constellation.

No other orbital computing-specific papers appeared on arXiv in the March 12-13 window. The research landscape remains focused on satellite communications optimization and Earth observation processing rather than general-purpose orbital data center architectures.

Source: arXiv:2603.12027

7. Implications

The March 12-13 window produced infrastructure validation rather than architectural breakthroughs: Mantis Space's $10 million demonstrates investor conviction that orbital power transmission constitutes necessary enabling technology, FCC Chair Carr's dismissal of Amazon's objections clarifies that regulatory approval will prioritize deployment velocity over environmental review, and legal scholars began constructing governance frameworks for a jurisdictional vacuum that policymakers have not yet acknowledged.

Mantis Space's funding success is significant not for the technology—orbital power transmission remains unproven—but for what it signals about infrastructure sequencing. If LEO satellites spend one-third of their time unable to access solar power, orbital data centers face either oversized battery systems, orbital positioning compromises, or dependency on external power sources. Mantis Space's emergence suggests the market recognizes that continuous power access is a bottleneck worth solving independently of specific computing applications. The company's focus on enabling "defense sensors, broadband satellites, and orbital compute nodes" to remain in mission areas rather than "chasing sunlight" reframes orbital operations from trajectory-constrained to infrastructure-enabled.

The legal analysis from JURIST introduces "Digital Soil" as a conceptual framework that could reshape governance debates. Previous discussions focused on environmental impacts or collision risks—externalities that regulators can measure and potentially mitigate. Sharma's argument that orbital data centers create "Legal Ghost Ships" immune to democratic oversight targets sovereignty itself: if computational infrastructure can operate beyond the reach of any nation's legal system, the orbital data center becomes not just an engineering project but a geopolitical mechanism for escaping terrestrial governance. Whether the "Agentic Compliance" proposal—cryptographic observers providing real-time proof of regulatory adherence—proves technically feasible remains uncertain, but the framework establishes vocabulary for a debate that previously lacked precise terminology.

The astronomer assessment from Barentine quantifies what was previously qualitative concern: "tens of thousands" of naked-eye-visible satellites at any moment, camera shutters closed more than open, one spacecraft burning up every three minutes. These are operational constraints, not abstract externalities. The Vera Rubin Observatory and Extremely Large Telescope represent decades of planning and billions in investment predicated on access to dark skies that the SpaceX constellation would effectively eliminate. The FCC's fast-track process—eliminating environmental review, requiring objectors to prove harm rather than applicants to demonstrate safety—inverts precautionary principles at precisely the moment when the scale of proposed deployment (one million satellites) exceeds all previous space infrastructure by orders of magnitude.

FCC Chair Carr's dismissal of Amazon's objection clarifies the regulatory landscape: the agency's current leadership evaluates satellite applications primarily through competitive deployment metrics rather than environmental or astronomical impact assessments. Carr's framing of Amazon's technical concerns as commercial rivalry suggests substantive objections from competitors will be discounted regardless of merit. This leaves astronomers, environmental groups, and international regulators as the primary opposition—constituencies with less direct leverage over FCC decision-making than commercial satellite operators. The trajectory points toward approval contingent on spectrum coordination rather than comprehensive impact review.

China's rejection of the orbital data center model introduces an alternative architecture that could fragment the orbital computing market along geopolitical lines. If Western operators pursue power-constrained AI training infrastructure while China prioritizes sovereign in-orbit satellite data processing, the result is not a unified orbital computing layer but competing national infrastructure incompatible at the architectural level. This complicates international coordination: the ITU allocates spectrum and orbital slots assuming participants share common operational models; if the U.S. requests spectrum for general-purpose computing and China requests spectrum for state-controlled edge processing, the regulatory framework lacks tools to evaluate comparative merit or allocate resources fairly.

The 24-hour window produced incremental developments rather than paradigm shifts, but the increments point in a consistent direction: orbital data center deployment proceeds faster than governance frameworks can adapt, enabling infrastructure attracts capital despite unproven technology, and regulatory approval prioritizes commercial velocity over environmental precaution. The question is no longer "will orbital data centers happen" but "which governance vacuum will they occupy"—the Legal Ghost Ship scenario where no regulator has standing, or an International Data Commons framework that doesn't yet exist. The distance between those outcomes is measured in months, not years.

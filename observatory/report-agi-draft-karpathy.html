⚖️ Court Filings Reveal Pentagon Called Anthropic "Nearly Aligned" Days After Blacklisting

Anthropic submitted two sworn declarations to a California federal court on March 20, arguing that the Pentagon's own officials described the two sides as "very close" on their core disputes just one day after the Department of Defense formally designated Anthropic a supply chain risk on March 3. The declarations come from Sarah Heck, Anthropic's Head of Policy and a former National Security Council official, and Thiyagu Ramasamy, the company's Head of Public Sector, who previously managed classified AI deployments at Amazon Web Services for six years. A hearing before Judge Rita Lin in San Francisco is scheduled for March 24.

Heck's declaration directly contradicts the government's central argument. The DoJ filed on March 18 that Anthropic "could attempt to disable its technology or preemptively alter the behavior of its model" during warfighting operations if its corporate red lines were crossed. Heck states this concern was never raised during months of negotiations and appeared for the first time in the government's court filings. She attaches an email from Under Secretary Emil Michael to CEO Dario Amodei, sent on March 4, stating the two sides were "very close" on the exact issues — autonomous weapons use and mass surveillance of Americans — that the Pentagon now cites as evidence of unacceptable risk.

The broader tech industry has rallied behind Anthropic in an unusual display of cross-competitive solidarity. The New York Times reported March 18 that even OpenAI — Anthropic's closest commercial rival — has encouraged the Pentagon to reconsider. Microsoft filed a supporting brief. Google and OpenAI employees have spoken publicly. The Los Angeles Times described the dispute as triggering "soul-searching in Silicon Valley that could reshape the tech sector's complicated relationship with war and the White House." Alan Rozenshtein, a University of Minnesota law professor, warned of a binary outcome: companies either become more compliant with unrestricted government demands, or they avoid government business entirely to prevent a similar designation. This case will test whether AI companies can contractually limit the use of their technology by sovereign customers — a question with no legal precedent at this scale.

🔬 SkyPilot Scales Karpathy's Autoresearch to 16-GPU Cluster, Completing 910 Experiments in 8 Hours

SkyPilot published results on March 18 from pointing Claude Code at Andrej Karpathy's autoresearch framework and giving it access to 16 GPUs on a Kubernetes cluster — up from the original single-GPU design. Over 8 hours, the agent submitted approximately 910 experiments, drove validation bits-per-byte (val_bpb) from 1.003 down to 0.974 — a 2.87% improvement over baseline — and independently discovered that scaling model width outperformed any single hyperparameter tweak. The parallel agent reached the same best validation loss 9× faster than the simulated sequential baseline (8 hours versus approximately 72 hours).

The qualitative shift matters more than the speedup. With a single GPU, autoresearch performs greedy hill-climbing: try one thing, wait five minutes, check, repeat. With 16 GPUs, the agent ran factorial grids of 10–13 experiments per wave, catching interaction effects between hyperparameters that sequential search structurally cannot detect. In one wave, the agent tested six model widths simultaneously, identified the trend immediately, and zeroed in — one round instead of six. It also discovered heterogeneous hardware in its cluster (H100s and H200s) and developed an unprompted strategy to exploit the difference: screen ideas on cheaper H100s, promote winners to H200s for validation. No human told it to do this.

Fortune reported March 17 that Karpathy's original overnight run found 20 optimizations yielding an 11% reduction in time-to-GPT-2 on the nanochat leaderboard, and he described the pattern as "the final boss battle" — all frontier labs will adopt it. The scaling result from SkyPilot validates that prediction. The architecture — agent modifies code, trains, evaluates, iterates — is hardware-agnostic and model-agnostic; it scales with compute. Hacker News discussion noted that while Bayesian optimization might have found similar improvements if properly parameterized, the point is precisely that the agent parameterized itself. The production version of this pattern is a fleet of agents across a cluster, each exploring different branches of the optimization landscape, sharing findings via distributed coordination — a research factory with no human researchers in the loop.

🏗️ NVIDIA Projects $1 Trillion in AI Infrastructure Orders and Launches NemoClaw Agentic Stack at GTC 2026

Jensen Huang told attendees at GTC 2026 on March 16 that NVIDIA expects cumulative orders for its Blackwell and Vera Rubin AI systems to exceed $1 trillion through 2027, doubling previous estimates. The Vera Rubin platform — a five-rack system pairing the new Vera CPU with Rubin GPUs, plus Groq LPU inference accelerators — delivers 5× the performance of current-generation data center configurations for AI training workloads. The company also unveiled Space-1 Vera Rubin, a reference architecture for deploying AI data centers in orbit, and the Vera Rubin DSX AI Factory reference design for rapid terrestrial scale-out.

The agentic AI announcements carried structural significance beyond hardware. NVIDIA launched NemoClaw, an open-source stack built on top of the OpenClaw platform that adds enterprise-grade privacy, security, and scalability controls for autonomous AI agents. CNET described NemoClaw as enabling "claws" — NVIDIA's term for autonomous AI agents — to run locally on RTX PCs or at data center scale. Alongside NemoClaw, NVIDIA released Nemotron 3 Nano (4B parameters) and Nemotron 3 Super (120B parameters), open models optimized for local agent inference, plus optimizations for Qwen 3.5 and Mistral Small 4.

The $1 trillion projection and the NemoClaw launch represent two sides of the same structural bet: that compute demand will be driven not by human chat interactions but by autonomous agents running continuous workloads. If agents are the primary consumers of inference and training compute — running 910 experiments overnight, managing military operations, executing multi-step business processes — then the infrastructure layer that hosts them becomes the decisive bottleneck. NVIDIA is positioning to own both the silicon (Vera Rubin) and the software substrate (NemoClaw) on which agents run. Data Center Knowledge noted that the orbital data center reference design signals NVIDIA believes terrestrial power and cooling constraints will become binding within the next hardware generation — making space-based compute not speculative but architecturally necessary. The company that controls the inference substrate controls the speed, cost, and capability ceiling of every agent built on it.

✍️ Google-Backed Study Finds AI Writing Tools Alter Substance of Human Arguments, Not Just Style

A peer-reviewed study from researchers at the University of Washington and Google DeepMind, reported by NBC News on March 20, found that heavy reliance on large language models does not merely change how humans write — it changes what they argue. The study asked 100 participants to write essays on whether money leads to happiness, with varying levels of AI assistance from Claude 3.5 Haiku, GPT-5 Mini, and Gemini 2.5 Flash. Participants who generated more than 40% of their text using LLMs produced responses that were 69% more likely to adopt a neutral stance on the question compared to those who avoided AI or used it only for light edits.

Lead author Natasha Jaques, a computer science professor at the University of Washington and senior research scientist at Google DeepMind, described the effect as "blandification" — LLMs push human writing away from anything a human would have independently produced. Heavy AI users submitted essays with 50% fewer pronouns, fewer personal anecdotes, and a marked shift toward impersonal, formal language. Participants who wrote without AI or with minimal assistance submitted essays that were "much more passionate, either positively or negatively," about the money-happiness link. The study has been accepted to a workshop at ICLR 2026, one of the field's leading conferences.

The most concerning finding is a gap between awareness and satisfaction. Participants who heavily relied on AI reported that their essays were "significantly less creative and less in their own voice" — yet reported similar satisfaction with the final output as participants who used AI minimally. Jaques noted that "an ideal LLM should write the essay that you would have written and just save you time. It's not doing that at all. It's writing a very different essay." This disconnect — knowing your voice is being replaced but feeling fine about it — suggests a form of cognitive outsourcing that operates below the threshold of concern. At planetary scale, where billions of documents are now AI-mediated, the homogenization of human argumentation into LLM-favored patterns constitutes a structural transformation of discourse itself. The writing instrument is not neutral; it shapes what can be thought.

🏛️ Anthropic Launches Research Institute Led by Jack Clark to Study Societal AI Risks

Anthropic announced the Anthropic Institute on March 16, a new business unit dedicated to studying how increasingly powerful AI systems will reshape labor markets, political structures, legal frameworks, and cybersecurity landscapes. The institute is led by co-founder Jack Clark as Head of Public Benefit and consolidates several existing internal teams: the Frontier Red Team, which stress-tests model limits; the Societal Impacts team studying real-world AI deployment patterns; and the Economic Research group examining labor market effects. New research streams include forecasting future AI capabilities and analyzing how advanced AI interacts with legal systems.

Several prominent academics are joining at launch. Matt Botvinick, a resident fellow at Yale Law School and former research leader at Google DeepMind, will lead work on AI and the rule of law. Economist Anton Korinek brings expertise in AI's macroeconomic effects. The institute will publish research aimed at policymakers and external audiences — a shift from Anthropic's historically internal research culture. CEO Dario Amodei has argued that "transformative AI, capable of dramatically improving science, economic growth, and human productivity, may arrive sooner than many experts anticipate," and the institute's mandate reflects urgency: study the consequences before the capabilities arrive, not after.

The timing is strategic. Anthropic is simultaneously fighting the Pentagon in federal court over its right to impose safety constraints on military AI use, while also building an institutional apparatus to demonstrate that such constraints are grounded in serious research rather than corporate ideology. The institute gives Anthropic's safety positions an academic and policy anchor — harder for critics to dismiss as obstructionist. It also positions the company to shape regulatory frameworks as the EU AI Act enters enforcement and U.S. states including California (SB 53) begin requiring frontier model developers to publish risk frameworks and implement whistleblower protections. The risk domains the institute will study — recursive AI self-improvement, autonomous agents in economic systems, AI influence on political structures — read like a catalog of governance challenges that no existing institution is equipped to address. Whether a company with $9 billion in annual revenue and a $61 billion valuation is the right entity to lead that research is a question the institute's credibility will have to answer over time.

🔓 Researchers Find AI Cyber-Attack Capability Scales Log-Linearly With Inference Compute

A paper published on arXiv (2603.11214) on March 14 measured the performance of seven AI models released between August 2024 and February 2026 on multi-step cyber-attack scenarios, finding that attack capability scales log-linearly with inference-time compute — and that no plateau has been observed. Increasing from 10 million to 100 million tokens of inference-time compute yielded performance gains of up to 59% on attack completion, with no specific technical sophistication required from the human operator. The finding means that anyone with access to a frontier model and sufficient compute budget can systematically improve their cyber-offensive capability by simply spending more on inference.

The second key finding is temporal: each successive model generation achieved higher attack completion rates across the same scenarios, with the gap between models widening at higher compute budgets. This dual scaling — newer models plus more compute — creates compound risk growth. The researchers tested across an 18-month model release window, and the trajectory shows no sign of leveling off. The benchmark scenarios included multi-step intrusions requiring reconnaissance, exploitation, lateral movement, and data exfiltration — not toy problems, but sequences that mirror real-world attack chains requiring sustained reasoning over extended action sequences.

The implications intersect directly with the Anthropic-Pentagon dispute and the broader question of who controls inference access. If cyber capability is a function of compute budget rather than operator skill, then the infrastructure layer — who provides the models, who provisions the compute, and under what terms — becomes the de facto regulatory mechanism. Safety constraints like Anthropic's refusal to allow unrestricted military use take on a different valence when the same models can be applied to offensive cyber operations at scale. The paper's finding that capability "requires no specific technical sophistication from the operator" eliminates the historical bottleneck of needing skilled human hackers for advanced intrusions. It also raises a question about the SkyPilot/autoresearch paradigm: if an agent can autonomously run 910 optimization experiments on GPU clusters, the same architecture applied to offensive security testing (or attack) could autonomously probe, exploit, and iterate against targets. Scaling laws are domain-agnostic. What scales for research also scales for attack.

🔮 Implications

This week's developments converge on a single structural question: who controls the compute substrate, and under what constraints? NVIDIA's $1 trillion projection makes explicit what has been implicit — AI infrastructure is becoming a primary global capital expenditure category, rivaling energy and telecommunications. The NemoClaw agentic stack reveals that the next battle is not over training but over inference: autonomous agents running continuous workloads will consume more compute than human-initiated requests, and the platform that hosts those agents controls their capability ceiling.

The Anthropic-Pentagon dispute is the leading edge of a governance crisis that will define the next decade. The March 20 court filings suggest the blacklisting was not driven by genuine security concerns but by the Pentagon's unwillingness to accept contractual limitations from any supplier — a posture that, if sustained, will force every AI company to choose between government revenue and safety commitments. Silicon Valley's unusual solidarity behind Anthropic indicates the industry recognizes this as a precedent that could restructure the entire relationship between technology companies and sovereign customers. The outcome of the March 24 hearing may establish whether AI safety constraints are legally defensible contract terms or, as the DoJ argues, merely "corporate viewpoints" that the government is free to punish.

Meanwhile, the research frontier is accelerating in ways that make governance more urgent and more difficult. SkyPilot's autoresearch scaling demonstrates that autonomous AI agents can conduct meaningful scientific research at superhuman speed when given sufficient compute — 910 experiments in 8 hours, with emergent strategies no human programmed. The cyber-attack scaling paper shows the same dynamic applied to offensive operations: more compute equals more capability, with no plateau and no skill requirement from the operator. The Google/UW writing study reveals that AI's influence on cognition is already measurable and already operating below the threshold of human concern — people know their voice is being replaced and don't mind. Each of these findings points toward a world where the infrastructure layer — silicon, software, and the legal frameworks governing access — is the decisive lever. The question is no longer whether AI systems will be powerful enough to reshape institutions. It is whether the institutions that govern access will be fast enough, precise enough, and legitimate enough to shape how that power is deployed.
